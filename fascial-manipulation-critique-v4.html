Methodological close-read

Reading
between the lines.

A 2025 randomised trial reports a striking advantage for Fascial Manipulation® over eccentric training in patellar tendinopathy. Read alongside the literature it sits in — and against its own public pre-registration — the same trial tells a more complicated story.

The paper under review

Effectiveness of Fascial Manipulation® versus eccentric training with static stretching on patellar tendinopathy — a randomised clinical trial.

CTRI/2019/08/020643  ·  n = 88  ·  parallel RCT  ·  PubMed 41316610

Pre-registration is a public commitment: who will be studied, how they will be treated, and how success will be measured. Before any of that, though, every trial sits in a literature — and that literature shapes how its results should be read. Seven points, starting with the world this paper lives in. Each closes with what it means for a clinician reading critically.

01
The literature this paper sits in

A small literature, dominated by one network.

The entire PubMed-indexed literature on Fascial Manipulation® contains 73 papers across 17 years. That is a thin evidence base for a globally marketed, trademarked manual-therapy method, and it is concentrated in a way that should change how a clinician reads any single paper inside it — including this one.

What this means

When roughly half the literature on a proprietary, trademarked technique is co-authored by the same group that invented and licenses it, you have a structural conflict of interest no individual disclosure statement can fix. Independent replication should be the dominant mode by year seventeen; here it is the minority mode.

Most trials are also delivered by therapists certified inside the same school whose theoretical model is being tested, making allegiance and expectancy effects plausible across the whole evidence base. The paper we are about to examine is one square in this grid.

02
Headline conclusion

The two arms were not given comparable treatment packages.

The paper concludes that Fascial Manipulation® is superior on number of treatment sessions and on patient satisfaction. But this is not a clean comparison of two equivalent packages. The eccentric-training arm received a fixed programme of five sessions per week for four weeks. The Fascial Manipulation® arm received individualised treatment, between one and four sessions, with treatment ending when the therapist judged that pain and densification had resolved and movement was pain-free.

What this means

When one treatment stops the moment the patient improves and the other continues for twenty sessions regardless, of course the first will "require fewer sessions" — that is a property of the design, not the technique. And when one hour of individualised hands-on care is compared with a repetitive squat exercise, "higher satisfaction" is the predictable outcome. The two headline conclusions of the paper are decided in advance by the methodology.

03
Protocol drift

The published trial is not the trial that was registered.

The eligibility criteria, the exclusion list, the age range, the minimum symptom duration, and the dose of the intervention all changed between the public pre-registration and the published paper. None of these changes is flagged as a protocol amendment.

What this means

Pre-registration exists to prevent the question from being tailored to the answer. When the protocol shifts quietly, we as readers lose the ability to know whether the finding is what the trial set out to test or what was discovered afterwards.

04
Baseline imbalance

Randomisation produced groups that were not comparable.

Despite random allocation, the two groups began the trial in significantly different states on three of the four outcome scales — VISA-P, pressure-pain threshold, and tendon thickness. Only the NPRS pain score was balanced. The standard analytic correction for the imbalance — adjusting for baseline values — was not applied.

What this means

A group starting from a different baseline has more or less room to move on the outcome scale, and the comparison between groups is biased accordingly. This isn't merely a statistical issue — it directly distorts the size of the effect we read in the tables.

Evidence

Despite block randomisation with sealed opaque envelopes, the two arms differed substantially before any treatment was given. The authors cite Bland & Altman to argue that baseline p-values shouldn't matter in a randomised trial. The argument is correct in principle — and exactly why the imbalance here is uncomfortable. With groups this far apart at the start, the appropriate analysis is ANCOVA with baseline as a covariate, which absorbs the imbalance into the model. The paper instead compares post-treatment means and change scores, which are the most sensitive to it.

05
Effect size

The reported effect size is too large to be real.

The authors report a Cohen's d of 7.2 for pain reduction. For context, a d of 0.8 is conventionally considered large. A number this size is not seen in clinical trials of physiotherapy interventions, and likely indicates that the formula was applied with the wrong denominator.

What this means

If the effect size we cite is inflated several-fold, it is not comparable to effect sizes elsewhere in the literature and does not translate into a coherent clinical decision. When the number comes out implausible, the first to be sceptical should be the clinician about to apply it to their patients.

Evidence

Effect sizes above about 3 are unusual outside comparisons of populations that barely overlap. Values in this range strongly suggest the formula divided the mean change by the standard deviation of the change, rather than by the pooled standard deviation. The methods do not specify which was used.

A separate concern points in the same direction. Across the FM arm's three repeated-measures ANOVAs, the reported denominator degrees of freedom imply three different sample sizes within the same paper — VISA-P is consistent with n ≈ 15, while NPRS and PPT are consistent with n ≈ 88. The trial enrolled roughly 43 per arm. These three numbers cannot all be correct.

06
Arithmetic

Several rows in the published tables don't add up.

The published tables contain plain numerical errors: a satisfaction column that sums to over 113%, a confidence interval whose upper bound is below its lower bound, a tenfold asymmetry in the reported standard deviation of the same ultrasound measurement, and reported loss-to-follow-up of zero at twelve months — something that, as anyone working in physio knows, simply doesn't happen in practice.

What this means

These are errors that should have been caught in proofing — by the authors, the reviewers, and the editor. When none of them were caught, the probability that the rest of the paper was checked carefully drops, and we are obliged to read the remainder with a more cautious eye.

07
Disclosure

There is no competing-interests statement.

One co-author is the daughter of the inventor of the technique under test. The first author teaches certification courses in the technique and personally delivered all of the Fascial Manipulation® treatment in the trial. None of this is disclosed.

Competing interests — verbatim, page 9

The authors declare that they have no known competing financial interests or personal relationships that could have appeared to influence the work reported in this paper.

Sannasi et al., J Bodyw Mov Ther 45 (2025) 483–492

What this means

Intellectual and institutional conflicts of interest shape which questions are asked, what comparators are chosen, and how conclusions are framed — no less than financial ones. They do not make a finding wrong, but they are information the reader is entitled to receive openly in order to see the larger picture.

Notes

Most journals' disclosure policies — including ICMJE — explicitly include non-financial relationships of this kind. The earlier supportive evidence cited in the paper's introduction also originates substantially from the inventor's research group. That context belongs in the disclosure statement and in the reader's mental model of how much external corroboration the result has.

Both arms improved. The headline didn't.

The most defensible reading of this trial is not that Fascial Manipulation® beats eccentric training. It is that both treatments improved outcomes in young athletic patients over twelve months, that the groups began the trial unequal and ended it similar, and that several of the numbers reporting that outcome do not survive close inspection.

The deeper lesson is general. A small literature dominated by the network that licenses its own technique cannot be checked by reading one of its papers in isolation. Pre-registration is only useful when reviewers and readers actually check the published paper against it. The cost of doing so is an afternoon. The cost of not doing so accumulates in the literature.
